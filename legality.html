Duplication Legality

The protection of software started after it was realized that plagiarists could Escape copyright infringement through immaterial forms of theft. This was remedied by the passage of the Copyright Acts of 1976 and the adjustments made in 1980 by CONTU (The Congressional Commission on New Technological Uses on Copyrighted Works). Non-literal forms of infringement are different from direct infringement in that they mimic a concept rather than the actual language. Non-literal infringement was still present in the 1900s and courts were tasked with distinguish between idea and expression. However, software duplication is typically literal due to it being functional rather than showy. In 1978 it was decided that software wouldn’t be protected from non-literal infringement because the arrangement and combination is the also the intellectual property involved. Copyright isn’t an international law, it is often said that owning an idea is a very western idea, and thus isn’t enforced in many Asian countries. Software piracy in Taiwan alone resulted in a loss of 756 million dollars to the U.S. for software piracy in 1990. In the same year the U.S. software industry lost 400 million dollars to China for software piracy.
